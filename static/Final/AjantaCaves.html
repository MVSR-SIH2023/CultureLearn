Ajanta Caves


Introduction

The Ajanta Caves are 29 rock-cut Buddhist cave monuments dating from the second century BCE to about 480 CE in the Aurangabad District of Maharashtra state in India. Ajanta Caves are a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Universally regarded as masterpieces of Buddhist religious art, the caves include paintings and rock-cut sculptures described as among the finest surviving examples of ancient Indian art, particularly expressive paintings that present emotions through gesture, pose and form. The caves were built in two phases, the first starting around the second century BCE and the second occurring from 400 to 650 CE, according to older accounts, or in a brief period of 460–480 CE according to later scholarship. The Ajanta Caves constitute ancient monasteries (Viharas) and worship-halls (Chaityas) of different Buddhist traditions carved into a 75-metre (246 ft) wall of rock. The caves also present paintings depicting the past lives and rebirths of the Buddha, pictorial tales from Aryasura's Jatakamala, and rock-cut sculptures of Buddhist deities. Textual records suggest that these caves served as a monsoon retreat for monks, as well as a resting site for merchants and pilgrims in ancient India. While vivid colours and mural wall paintings were abundant in Indian history as evidenced by historical records, Caves 1, 2, 16 and 17 of Ajanta form the largest corpus of surviving ancient Indian wall-paintings. The Ajanta Caves are mentioned in the memoirs of several medieval-era Chinese Buddhist travellers. They were covered by jungle until accidentally "discovered" and brought to Western attention in 1819 by a colonial British officer Captain John Smith on a tiger-hunting party. The caves are in the rocky northern wall of the U-shaped gorge of the river Waghur, in the Deccan plateau. Within the gorge are a number of waterfalls, audible from outside the caves when the river is high. With the Ellora Caves, Ajanta is one of the major tourist attractions of Maharashtra. It is about 59 kilometres (37 miles) from the city of Jalgaon, Maharashtra, India, 104 kilometres (65 miles) from the city of Aurangabad, and 350 kilometres (220 miles) east-northeast of Mumbai.[8][20] Ajanta is 100 kilometres (62 miles) from the Ellora Caves, which contain Hindu, Jain and Buddhist caves, the last dating from a period similar to Ajanta. The Ajanta style is also found in the Ellora Caves and other sites such as the Elephanta Caves, Aurangabad Caves, Shivleni Caves and the cave temples of Karnataka.

History

  1. Origins (2nd century BCE - 1st century CE) -

    The Ajanta Caves date back to the 2nd century BCE, with the earliest caves believed to have been excavated during the Satavahana dynasty. The caves were initially used as simple viharas (monastic retreats) for Buddhist monks.

  2. Hinayana and Mahayana Phases (2nd century BCE - 6th century CE) -

    The caves went through different construction phases over several centuries. Initially, the caves followed Hinayana Buddhism, but later caves embraced Mahayana Buddhism. The transition is evident in the art and architecture of the caves.

  3. Abandonment (6th century CE) -

    Around the 6th century CE, the Ajanta Caves were gradually abandoned. There are various theories about the reasons, including the decline of Buddhism in India, a shift in political patronage, and environmental factors.

  4. Rediscovery (19th century) -

    The Ajanta Caves remained hidden for centuries, buried under thick vegetation. In 1819, a British officer named John Smith rediscovered the caves accidentally during a hunting expedition. The rediscovery brought international attention to the site.

  5. European Interest and Preservation (19th - 20th centuries) -

    European scholars and archaeologists, including James Fergusson and Alexander Cunningham, played significant roles in documenting and preserving the Ajanta Caves. The intricate murals and sculptures found in the caves became subjects of scholarly study.

  6. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1983) -

    In 1983, the Ajanta Caves were designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing their cultural and historical significance. The site attracts tourists, historians, and art enthusiasts from around the world.

  7. Cave Architecture and Art -

    The Ajanta Caves consist of both viharas (monasteries) and chaitya halls (prayer halls). The caves are renowned for their beautiful murals and sculptures, depicting scenes from the life of Buddha, Jataka tales, and various deities. The artists used natural pigments and created masterpieces of ancient Indian art

  8. Themes in the Murals -

    The murals in the Ajanta Caves cover a range of themes, including the life of Buddha, Bodhisattvas, celestial beings, courtly life, and nature. The artists achieved a high level of sophistication and expressiveness in their depictions.

Architecture

The architecture of the Ajanta Caves is a remarkable testament to ancient Indian craftsmanship and ingenuity. Carved into the horseshoe-shaped rock cliffs of the Waghora River valley in Maharashtra, these caves showcase an intricate blend of religious, artistic, and architectural excellence. Dating back to the 2nd century BCE and extending over several centuries, the caves consist of both viharas (monasteries) and chaitya halls (prayer halls). The architects meticulously carved the monastic caves into the rock face, creating spacious halls adorned with intricately carved pillars and doorways. The chaitya halls, designed for congregational worship, feature ribbed roofs imitating wooden structures, with an impressive stupa at the far end. The facades of the caves display ornate sculptures and reliefs, depicting scenes from the life of Buddha, various Bodhisattvas, and other mythological narratives. The architects employed a keen understanding of rock mechanics, utilizing the natural contours of the rock to enhance the aesthetic appeal. The precision in carving, attention to detail, and the integration of religious symbolism make Ajanta Caves an architectural marvel, providing insights into the artistic and spiritual aspirations of ancient India. The caves' architecture not only served practical purposes for the monastic community but also transcended into a canvas for expressing the profound narratives of Buddhism through the language of stone.

Worship Halls

The other type of main hall architecture is the narrower rectangular plan with high arched ceiling type chaitya-griha – literally, "the house of stupa". This hall is longitudinally divided into a nave and two narrower side aisles separated by a symmetrical row of pillars, with a stupa in the apse. The stupa is surrounded by pillars and concentric walking space for circumambulation. Some of the caves have elaborate carved entrances, some with large windows over the door to admit light. There is often a colonnaded porch or verandah, with another space inside the doors running the width of the cave. The oldest worship halls at Ajanta were built in the 2nd to 1st century BCE, the newest ones in the late 5th century CE, and the architecture of both resembles the architecture of a Christian church, but without the crossing or chapel chevette. The Ajanta Caves follow the Cathedral-style architecture found in still older rock-cut cave carvings of ancient India, such as the Lomas Rishi Cave of the Ajivikas near Gaya in Bihar dated to the 3rd century BCE. These chaitya-griha are called worship or prayer halls. The four completed chaitya halls are caves 9 and 10 from the early period, and caves 19 and 26 from the later period of construction. All follow the typical form found elsewhere, with high ceilings and a central "nave" leading to the stupa, which is near the back, but allows walking behind it, as walking around stupas was (and remains) a common element of Buddhist worship (pradakshina). The later two have high ribbed roofs carved into the rock, which reflect timber forms, and the earlier two are thought to have used actual timber ribs and are now smooth, the original wood presumed to have perished. The two later halls have a rather unusual arrangement (also found in Cave 10 at Ellora) where the stupa is fronted by a large relief sculpture of the Buddha, standing in Cave 19 and seated in Cave 26. Cave 29 is a late and very incomplete chaitya hall. The form of columns in the work of the first period is very plain and un-embellished, with both chaitya halls using simple octagonal columns, which were later painted with images of the Buddha, people and monks in robes. In the second period columns were far more varied and inventive, often changing profile over their height, and with elaborate carved capitals, often spreading wide. Many columns are carved over all their surface with floral motifs and Mahayana deities, some fluted and others carved with decoration all over, as in cave 1.


Paintings

he paintings in the Ajanta Caves are a treasure trove of ancient Indian art, providing a vivid glimpse into the rich cultural and religious tapestry of the time. Here are some key aspects of the paintings in the Ajanta Caves:

  1. Themes and Subjects -
    • The paintings predominantly revolve around Jataka tales, which are stories about the previous lives of Buddha.
    • Scenes from the life of Buddha, Bodhisattvas, and various divinities are beautifully depicted on the cave walls.
  2. Technique and Style -
    • The frescoes are executed in a classical Indian painting style, using natural pigments derived from minerals, plants, and stones.
    • Artists employed a fresco technique, where paintings were created on wet plaster, allowing for vibrant and enduring colors.
  3. Color Palette -
    • The color palette includes a wide range of hues, with rich reds, greens, blues, yellows, and whites dominating the compositions.
    • The artists skillfully used shading and tonal variations to bring depth and realism to the paintings.
  4. Aesthetic Detail -
    • The level of detail in the paintings is remarkable, capturing intricate jewelry, clothing patterns, and facial expressions.
    • The artists demonstrated a keen understanding of human anatomy and perspective, showcasing their mastery of the craft.
  5. Religious Significance -
    • The paintings served a dual purpose – to adorn the cave interiors and to convey the teachings of Buddhism in a visually engaging manner.
    • They played a crucial role in educating the monastic community and pilgrims about the life and virtues of Buddha.
  6. Preservation Challenges -
    • Over the centuries, exposure to the elements has taken a toll on the paintings, and efforts have been made for their preservation.
